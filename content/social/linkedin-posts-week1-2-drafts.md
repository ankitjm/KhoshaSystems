## POST 1 — Week 1, Tuesday
**Pillar:** Indian Retail Insights (Educational)
**Format:** Text post (~1,400 characters)

---

The average Indian electronics retailer loses ₹2–3 lakh every month.

Not to competition. Not to slow sales. To inventory they can't track.

Here's how it happens:

A mid-size telecom shop manages 500–1,000 active devices at any time. Each device has a unique IMEI number. But most retailers track inventory at the SKU level — "Samsung Galaxy S26, qty: 12."

The problem? Two Samsung Galaxy S26 units are not the same. Each has a unique identity that matters for warranty, GST audits, brand scheme claims, and returns.

When you track at SKU level, shrinkage hides in plain sight.

A device gets moved between stores — no record.
A return gets processed but the IMEI doesn't match — no flag.
A scheme claim deadline passes because nobody knew that specific unit qualified.

Multiply this across 30–50 active brand schemes and 3–5 store locations. The leakage adds up fast.

Most retailers don't even know they're losing this money. They see it as "normal wastage" or "the cost of doing business."

It's not. It's a systems problem. And it's solvable.

We've seen retailers cut shrinkage by 70%+ just by moving from SKU-level to IMEI-level tracking. No magic — just treating each device as unique from the moment it enters the store to the moment it leaves.

If you run an electronics or telecom retail business in India, here's a question worth asking: do you know exactly where every device in your inventory is right now?

## POST 2 — Week 1, Thursday
**Pillar:** Founder Journey / Building in Public
**Format:** Text post (~1,300 characters)

---

Before we wrote a single line of code, we spent 3 months sitting in electronics shops.

Not as customers. As observers.

We'd show up at opening time and stay until close. Watching how billing worked. How inventory was counted. How brand scheme claims were filed. How customers were handled during rush hour.

Here's what surprised us:

The billing process at most shops took 5–7 minutes per sale. Not because the staff was slow — because the systems were. Lookup the IMEI. Check the scheme. Calculate GST. Generate the invoice. Verify warranty registration. All on different systems. Some on paper.

Scheme management was chaos. Retailers were juggling 30–50 active brand schemes across Samsung, Vivo, Oppo, Xiaomi — each with different rules, different deadlines, different claim processes. Most tracked them in Excel. Many missed deadlines and left lakhs in payouts unclaimed.

The real eye-opener: the most profitable retailers weren't always the ones with the best locations or the highest footfall. They were the ones who had figured out operational efficiency — even if their "system" was a notebook and a sharp memory.

That's when we knew what to build. Not a POS system. Not an ERP. A purpose-built operating system for how Indian electronics retail actually works.

Three years later, that's RetailerOS.

Sometimes the best product research is just sitting in the shop and watching.

## POST 3 — Week 2, Tuesday
**Pillar:** Customer Pain Points & Solutions
**Format:** Text post (~1,200 characters)

---

A retailer in Hyderabad was losing ₹1.5 lakh every month.

He had no idea.

He ran 3 stores. Good locations. Decent footfall. Revenue looked fine on the surface. But his margins kept shrinking and he couldn't figure out why.

Here's what was happening:

His staff was processing 40+ returns a month. Normal for electronics retail. But without IMEI-level tracking, nobody verified whether the returned device was the same one that was sold. Some weren't.

He was eligible for 35+ brand schemes at any time. His manager tracked them in a shared Google Sheet. By the time most claims were filed, the deadlines had passed. He was leaving 10–15% of eligible payouts on the table. Every single month.

His inventory counts were off by 8–12% because inter-store transfers were tracked manually — sometimes on WhatsApp, sometimes not at all.

None of these problems showed up as a single big loss. They showed up as a slow bleed — ₹40K here from missed schemes, ₹30K there from shrinkage, ₹20K from GST corrections.

Added up: ₹1.5 lakh/month. ₹18 lakh/year.

The fix wasn't complicated. It was systematic: IMEI-level tracking, automated scheme alerts, real-time inter-store inventory sync.

How much is your business losing to problems you can't see?

## POST 4 — Week 2, Thursday
**Pillar:** Contrarian Takes
**Format:** Text post (~1,100 characters)

---

Stop calling Tally a POS system.

I see this constantly — electronics retailers in India running their entire operation on Tally and calling it their "POS."

Tally is an accounting tool. A very good one. But it was never designed to:

→ Track individual devices by IMEI number
→ Manage 30+ brand schemes with different rules and deadlines
→ Handle multi-store inventory in real time
→ Generate GST-compliant invoices with automatic HSN codes
→ Sync warranty registrations at point of sale
→ Alert you when a scheme claim deadline is 3 days away

Using Tally as your POS is like using Excel as your CRM. It kind of works — until it really doesn't.

The gap isn't Tally's fault. It's a category mismatch. Accounting software solves accounting problems. Retail operations need retail operations software.

Indian electronics retail has specific, complex workflows — IMEI tracking, brand scheme management, GST compliance, multi-store coordination. These aren't edge cases. They're the core of the business.

The question isn't "is Tally enough?" — it's "what am I leaving on the table by forcing a tool to do something it wasn't built for?"
